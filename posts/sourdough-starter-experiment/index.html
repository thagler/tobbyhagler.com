Sourdough Starter Experiment

I've finally found some great formulas with sourdough starters that work for me.

I've experimented with sourdough starters before, but they ultimately fail because of neglect or impatience. One of my latest sets was no exception. I had two nearly-viable starters after several weeks of feeding (the first was over a month) with still no luck. I tried feeding variations—different ratios, hydration levels, and flour mixes—but only ever saw a slight rise from them. Eventually, I inadvertently killed them by leaving them in the oven with the light on for too long, and they spoiled.

Based on what I've learned about sourdough chemistry (the yeast, the LAB, how feeding and hydration ratios make a difference, here's what finally worked for me. I'm currently growing two amazing starters after only two days.

Tips for the noobs

Sourdough requires a kitchen scale. Ratios are based on weight, not volume, so ditch anything that uses the ambiguous "ounce" as a measurement. Convert to grams—this is science, after all, which requires the metric system.

Ratios are expressed as starter-to-flour-to-water, or X:Y:Z. A good practice is to place a clean jar on your scale and tare it to zero. Pour 20 grams of your spent starter into the jar and discard the rest (pro-tip: search for "sourdough discard pancake recipe"). Early days of a starter call for a 1:1:1 ratio: 20 grams of spent starter, 20 grams of water, and 20 grams of flour. Stir well, cover loosely, and let it sit.

Measure the weight of your empty jars beforehand. You'll thank me later.

Another tip is to use an old hair band or a rubber band to mark the level. This way you can see how high it rises over time. Dry erase markers also work well.

Starter #1: Zymia

Yeast water + rye and wheat flour.

A week prior, I started some yeast water (originally intended to feed one of the previous starters that was struggling). This consists of a handful of raisins in a jar of filtered water from the fridge and a tablespoon of honey. Stir, shake, and let sit for a week. When it's slightly fizzy when shaken, and before the raisin skins flake off, it's ready. It'll have a slightly sweet, hard cider-like smell to it, and a light, golden-brown color that's not cloudy.

My starter consisted of 30 grams of whole wheat flour and 30 grams of yeast water; a 1:1 starter ratio.

A day later, it rose a little before falling. When I fed it, I didn't discard anything. Instead, I tared the still-full jar and removed it, giving me the weight of the starter once I added the jar's empty weight. In my case, it was around 60 grams of starter. I fed it at a ratio of 1:1:1—60 grams of the spent starter, 60 grams of wheat flour, and 60 grams of yeast water.

The next day, it had nearly doubled in height, with lots of hardy bubbles and a decent dome. That's impressive in only two days.

And it smells so sweet. The familiar sourdough tang is there, along with a cider aroma that's so unique to the yeast water.

Starter #2: Rhyza

This one was more traditional. It was a simple 1:1 ratio of 20 grams of rye flour and 10 grams of wheat flour, and 30 grams of filtered water. The first feeding was a 1:1:1 ratio of all 60 grams of the spent starter, 40 grams of rye flour mixed with 20 grams of wheat flour, and 60 grams of filtered water.

At the same time as Zymia's nearly doubled rise, Rhyza had almost tripled in height! I was almost afraid it would expand out of the jar. Thankfully, it reached its peak just before reaching the lid.

Next steps

I'll update this blog over time with the progress of these experiments. So far, they're off to great starts.

I will discard and feed them again in the morning, using 1:1:1 of their respective makeup, and re-evaluate in the evening. I will discard all but 20 grams of the starter, so my flour and water will also be 20 grams each.

With the discard of tomorrow evening's feeding (whichever seems the most active), I will make bread. I'll start by feeding the discard with a 1:0.5:0.5 ratio and using the resulting levain to start the bread dough. I estimate that the bread dough will take 8-10 hours, so I'll let it rise overnight. 

If it's successful, I'll post the recipe.

Wish me luck!
