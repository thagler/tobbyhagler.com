Baking Adjustments for My House

Every home has a climate. Mine? It’s the chaotic swamp ecosystem known as South Carolina. Warm and humid most of the year, cool and dry when the HVAC gets freaked out by the occasional cold snap.

Sourdough recipes aren’t universal; they’re suggestions. My house runs warm, so yeast goes full Fast & Furious during bulk fermentation. My house is also humid, so I tend to adjust the starter feed ratios by at least an extra point for flour. If I follow most timing instructions, I end up with overproofed dough, asking why I betrayed it.

Solutions I’ve learned the hard way:

What I adjust because of humidity

  • I start a little lower on hydration than the recipe suggests
    (e.g., 70% hydration might behave like 78% here)
  • I start with flour conservatively
    • I can always bassinage in some moisture if I went too conservative
  • I start slow with an autolyse before adding active starter (and then salt)

What I adjust because of temperature

  • Bulk fermentation often needs less time
  • Proofing sometimes needs the fridge as a timeout space
  • My starter (Rhyza especially) ferments faster and wants more frequent feedings

What I adjust because “life happens”

  • If the dough is telling me it’s ready, I move to the next step
    (clocks don’t tell me when to bake bread, the dough does)
  • If family plans shift, cold proof becomes my safety net

Once I stopped blaming myself for dough that spread like a tired pancake, and started working with my actual environment, things got a lot easier. Your kitchen isn’t a lab. It’s an ecosystem. Learn its quirks, and your bread will start behaving like it belongs there.

How to Figure Out Your Home’s Dough Personality

1) Measure dough temperature after mixing

Use an instant-read thermometer and check the center of the dough. What the number means:

  • 70–75°F (21–24°C) → Normal fermentation speed
  • 76–82°F (24–28°C) → Yeast is turbocharged, bulk will move faster
  • Below 70°F (21°C) → Dough will ferment slowly and may need more time

How to adjust:

  • Too warm? Use colder water next time. Chill between folds.
  • Too cold? Warmer water for mixing. Use a proofing box:
    • The oven with only a light on works wonders.
    • The microwave with a mug of hot water also works.

2) Watch how fast your starter peaks after a feeding

Peak = when the dome stops rising and just begins to level. Timing clues:

  • 4–6 hours > Fermentation moves fast > expect shorter bulk
  • 8–10 hours > Moderate speed > recipe timing likely holds
  • 12+ hours > Starter is slow > will be a longer bulk

Rule of thumb: Bulk fermentation should roughly match your starter’s peak behavior.

3) Compare hydration to behavior, not the recipe

Hydration is a feel, not a bragging right. If dough is:

  • Super sticky and puddles out > Gluten underdeveloped or humidity high > lower water 5–10% next time
  • Tight and tears easily > Add a little water during folds (bassinage style)

Write down your climate:

  • High-humidity homes usually land 5–8% lower hydration than online recipes
  • Dry climates might need +3–5% more water

4) Log your actual bulk times

Forget the recipe the second your dough starts telling the truth. A good bulk is:

  • Dough is 50–70% larger
  • Smooth surface
  • Bubbles visible at the edges
  • Gentle poke springs back slowly

This becomes the basis for your recipe.

5) Notice how quickly skin forms on the dough

This is humidity’s role in shaping. If surface skin:

  • Forms fast > House is dry > Cover more, mist dough lightly
  • Never forms > House is humid > Take advantage for shaping… but reduce hydration
