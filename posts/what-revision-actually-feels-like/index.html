What Revision Actually Feels Like

Surprisingly, revisions turned out to be one of my favorite parts of writing *The Grove of Tau Ceti*. It was the moment of "ugh, my draft sucks" to "oh, maybe this thing has some life inside it, yet."

Terry Pratchett once (and often) said, "The first draft is just you telling yourself the story." And he was absolutely correct.

Revision is the act of opening the machine you built and discovering the screws you thought were decorative are load-bearing. And that the most critical moments aren't actually all that important. With The Grove of Tau Ceti, the hard part was the first draft: that sprint to the finish line, the calendar looming on the wall, the “don’t break the chain” word counts.

Once I knew (really knew) the story, I needed others to read it and give me feedback -- beta readers to whom I cannot extend a large enough "thank you!" to. Because of them, I could see all the plot holes, the developmental flaws, the good parts and the really terrible ones.

What surprised me is that revisions became one of my favorite parts of making this book. There’s a moment, the shift from “ugh, this draft is mush” to “hol up… there’s a heartbeat under here.” It’s addictive.

The story starts talking back. Characters sharpen and begin to take over. A scene that used to limp suddenly stands upright and says, “You meant to write this.” Revision is when the book proves it actually wants to live.

Characters misbehaving turned out to be the biggest signal that needed strengthening

V didn't have enough agency in the beginning. I had this idea of where I wanted the story to go, but V had other ideas. Revising the story gave me the chance to actually listen to her and let her make her own decisions -- decisions that shaped the story and led to its only logical conclusion.

Parker slipping into self-loathing (early first drafts were very "woe is me") wasn’t a choice he would make. It was a mistake I made when drafting fast. I had to restore his swagger, make sure he remembered he once called himself "the most dangerous human in Tau Ceti," and then act like it.

Rowan needed to shine earlier; his technical prowess and guilt over a past career-limiting incident needed to be clearer. Hawthorne was too guarded early on, and the lack of any vulnerability made her difficult to appreciate. Demeter's “voice” evolved from a passive computer system to that of a true character. The captain was way too casual and commanded no actual authority. Revisions allowed me to sprinkle in notes of humanity and to fix details, revealing the soul behind every archetypal portrayal.

Revision is making sure the people in a story don’t drift into two-dimensional cardboard cutouts. This is the moment to let them really come alive on the page, to let them become real.

And then...

...then it's off to the editor. That's the nail-biting part: what will my editor tell me, and how bad will it be...?
