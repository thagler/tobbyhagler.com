On Starting Things Before You’re Ready

Stop sharpening pencils. That wears them down faster than writing with them.

There’s this myth that preparedness is a gate you pass through, like once you’ve read enough articles or watched enough bakers coil-fold dough on YouTube (watching slap folds is my favorite, but it's never as easy as they make it look), a bell dings, and you’re licensed to begin.

In real life, readiness is usually something you discover halfway through messing something up. My first loaf didn’t care that I’d spent a week “researching hydration percentages.” It just sat there like a beige swamp creature until the oven convinced it to commit to being bread. Starting is what creates the readiness, not the other way around.

It's the same with writing. We can outline ourselves into a paralysis spiral, convinced one more note will finally unlock the perfect first sentence. Meanwhile, the draft is over there, coughing loudly, like “Hey, I exist when you actually type something.”

You don’t learn the shape of a story by thinking about it in the abstract. You learn it by letting sentences wobble around until they find their center of gravity. Premature perfection is a hobby for machines, not humans. And machines can't write or 

So: start before you think you're qualified.

Bake when your starter still smells edgy. Write when the keyboard is stiff, or the pencil is still dull. Your Muse will not make house calls. You have to go find her. Begin while you're still slightly confused and underproofed. Just keep going until the words begin to rise.
