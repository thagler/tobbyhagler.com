Notes From a Bad Bake

Sourdough is applied chemistry, but I am very bad at chemistry. Every baker has a file labeled “Well… that didn’t go according to plan.” Mine is a three-ring binder.

Sometimes the starter is sleepy. Sometimes the weather is being passive-aggressive. Sometimes my brain insists that 90 percent hydration is a personality trait. A “bad bake” usually starts as good intentions plus one tiny decision that seemed brilliant until it wasn’t.

I'm still learning a ton about baking with sourdough. An autolyse dough rise is important most of the time. Hydration percentages account for additional ingredients (especially wet ones). Enriched dough doesn't play like artisan dough. There's a lot to learn.

But here’s perhaps the most valuable part I’ve had to learn: pivoting is a baking skill.

  • Weak dough?
    • Pan loaf it.
  • Overproofed?
    • Chill it, reshape it, and reset expectations.
  • Too sticky to shape?
    • Make focaccia.
  • Overhandled?
    • Dinner rolls.
  • Dough tearing like tissue paper?
    • Increase rest time and pretend that was always the plan.
  • If it has become goop that refuses to let go of your fingers?
    • Sorry, it wasn't meant to be, and either feed it like a starter or dump it into the trash. 

Most mistakes become something edible if you stop arguing with physics and let the loaf tell you what its options are.

I've had some interesting bakes that were rescues. There was the loaf that spread into a lazy pancake on the counter until I scooped it into a cast-iron skillet and baked it into accidentally perfect focaccia bread. Or the underproofed dough that refused to relax, so I divided it into small rolls and let surface tension do the heavy lifting. (Flattered dough shapes rise higher.)

I say this at work all the time (though I originally stole it from the Internet): "Good judgment is the result of experience; Experience is the result of bad judgment."

Here’s a baking corollary: a bake isn't “bad” if it teaches you something, and especially not if it feeds people.

You adjust, you try again, and you stack tiny lessons: wetter isn’t always better, impatience tastes dense, dough likes rest, and the oven forgives a lot. Besides, bread is a living thing. Some days it’s a cooperative friend. Other days, it’s a necromancer with opinions. You learn to work with both.

Pivot Menu: Full Panic-Button Playbook

If the dough is too sticky to shape:

  • Wet hands, not dry flour; this prevents gummy layers.
  • Do an extra coil fold for structure,
  • Switch to focaccia or pan loaf mode.
  • Cold rest 20–30 min to tighten gluten.
  • Scrape, don’t wrestle. The scraper always wins.

If the dough is tight and tears easily:

  • Mandatory rest. Gluten needs a moment.
  • Perform gentler, fewer folds.
  • Increase hydration a tiny bit next time.
  • Make rolls, and let surface tension do all the work.

If it’s overproofed and collapsing:

  • Chill it to slow the yeast down.
  • Very gentle reshape with minimal degassing.
  • Proof shorter the second time (watch, don’t time).
  • Accept the baguette-but-wider aesthetic.
  • French toast solves morale issues.

If it’s underproofed and stubbornly small:

  • Warm spot, covered, 15-minute check-ins.
  • Coil fold to wake up the gluten.
  • Be patient, gas buildup takes time.
  • Rebrand as “rustic, dense, European artisan vibes.”
  • This is a good time to practice your lame scoring techniques.

If the dough spread like a pancake:

  • Skillet bread to the rescue.
  • Sheet pan focaccia as Plan C.
  • Tell people it’s flatbread “on purpose.”

If shaping has gone feral:

  • Smaller divisions = easier tension.
  • Bench knife practice time.
  • Pretend you invented a new shape. Make knots out of smaller pieces.
  • Everything looks artisanal if you dust it with flour.
  • Pro-tip: using stencils while dusting with flour makes it look cool when baked.

If the crust looks great but the inside is gluey:

  • Bake longer next time. It wasn’t done.
  • Low-and-slow finish, if it’s salvageable.
  • Garlic butter forgives all sins.

If the dough has proofed for too long:

  • Treat it like an early active starter. 
  • Feed it some flour until it becomes handleable again.
    • Note: This will make it dry, once it autolyses.
  • This may be the opportunity to practice bassinage hydration.

If you forgot salt:

  • Garlic butter forgives this, too.
  • Serve with soup. No one ever complains about plain bread with soup.

If it tastes fine but looks tragic:

  • Close your eyes and take another bite.
  • Butter makes it prettier.
  • Bread isn’t a beauty pageant. Instagram doesn’t need to know everything.

The One Real Rule

Pivot early. Pivot creatively. Pivot without shame.
Just pivot!
