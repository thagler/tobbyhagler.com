The Dough Will Tell You

Sourdough is 66% science, 33% art, and 42% intuition. Baking with sourdough is even wonkier—it's sometimes easier to bake by feel than by recipe.

At some point in baking sourdough, you stop asking the clock what’s happening and start asking the dough. This sounds mystical until you realize it’s mostly about poking things and paying attention. Timers are useful, but dough doesn’t know what time it is. It knows temperature, hydration, gluten development, and whether you’ve been hovering over it like an anxious raccoon or a kid waiting for Santa to arrive.

The phrase “the dough will tell you” isn’t advice so much as a reminder that the thing you’re working with is right there, offering live telemetry, if you’re willing to read it. Oof. 

Early on, I wanted numbers: minutes, percentages, measurements. A spreadsheet that could tell me exactly when bulk fermentation was done. But sourdough refuses to be domesticated that way. The same formula behaves differently depending on the season, the kitchen, the starter’s mood, and whatever else is happening at a microbial level that I can’t see but deeply respect.

So instead of asking, “Has it been three hours?” I started asking questions that felt embarrassingly tactile: Does this feel lighter than it did an hour ago? Is it holding tension? Is it relaxing into the bowl like it’s had a long day? When I press it, does it push back, or does it sigh? 

Do I see bubbles?! (Oh, crap!)

The shift is subtle but important. You stop performing steps and start running diagnostics. Windowpane tests, poke tests, and coil folds that tell you more in ten seconds than a recipe ever will. It’s not intuition in the woo sense; it’s pattern recognition. You do the thing enough times, you mess it up enough times, and eventually your hands become a decent instrument. They're not perfect, just calibrated. The dough goes from opaque to conversational. You ask, and it answers. Sometimes rudely, but it answers.

This turns out to be useful outside the kitchen, which is annoying but true.

Writing does the same thing. Drafts don’t care how long you’ve been working on them. Scenes don’t respect your outline. Characters respect them even worse.

At some point, the work itself starts giving feedback, and your job is to notice it instead of forcing it to behave. The dough will tell you. The page will too. Neither is especially concerned with your schedule, but both are surprisingly generous if you’re willing to listen.
