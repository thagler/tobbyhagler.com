Starter Notes: Yours Isn’t Broken

If you’ve been baking sourdough longer than approximately three minutes, you’ve probably stared into a jar of flour-goop and thought: “This ecosystem of tiny, defenseless creatures has died and I am responsible.” You are not (*probably).

Starters are dramatic. They sulk. They're like thirsty peace lilies, swooning over the edge of their pot.  They change their mind about what they smell like. They go through phases like middle schoolers and microbial colonies tend to do. The good news: a “weird” starter is usually just a starter doing starter things (and figuring out who they are).

The biggest misconception is that starters follow rules. They don’t even follow suggestions. Same feeding schedule, same flour, same temperature… wildly different moods. So I’ve learned to treat mine less like a biological formula and more like a roommate I feed to keep the peace. The trick is noticing what it’s telling you instead of assuming failure every time it sends up a flare.

Here are a few common panic scenarios and the appropriate non-panic responses:

  • It smells like nail polish remover.
    Feed it. It just burned through its food supply, and that smell is the starter begging for seconds.
  • A thin layer of gray-ish liquid (“hooch”) on top.
    Stir it in and feed it. It's not the 9020s anymore—hooch is not a crime. Hooch is hydration with drama.
  • It’s not rising as much as it used to.
    Warm it up (a kitchen temp of 75–80°F helps) and give a couple of 1:2:2 feeds. If your kitchen isn't warm, let it rest one or two hours in the oven with the light on after feeding.
  • It smells weirdly cheesy or funky.
    Keep feeding. Don’t over-sniff. Early fermentation can get weird before it gets tasty.
  • It has bubbles but no dome.
    It’s just relaxed. Gluten structure changes with flour type and temperature. I should do a post sometime about the different flours I use for different structures and nutrients.
  • It looks lumpy or separates after feeding.
    Mix more thoroughly next time. The gluten just hasn’t run its morning coffee.
    Pro-tip: With a little bit of starter in the jar, add your water, swish it around, and stir until it's a white opaque liquid. Then add your flour and stir for 30+ seconds. Whip it into a cream. Even it out in the jar, and wrap a rubber band (or hair band) around that line of the jar; this marks your starting point from which you can measure its overall growth.

* And yes, if there’s pink, orange, bright red, or fuzzy horror-film growth?
That’s the one actual rule: throw that out and start over. In this case, it really is dead.
(If the mold is polka-dot cute, it’s still mold.)

Feed it, then neglect it.

Starters thrive when we stop micromanaging them. Feed consistently. Keep it somewhere warm-ish. Switch flours occasionally if you want to feel like a scientist:

  • Bread flour is your go-to. It's a decent mixture of nutrients and glutens.
  • Rye is great for food, but lousy for structure. It also soaks up water differently.
  • Whole wheat is good for managing gluten strength and slowing things down.
  • All-purpose flour is fine if you need to add bulk, but otherwise it's empty calories.

But you don’t need to helicopter-parent the yeast. If it’s bubbly, rising at least a little, and smells like something between bread and beer? It’s fine. Possibly even thriving. You’re doing great, by the way. And now you have one less anxious raccoon worry to carry into your day.
