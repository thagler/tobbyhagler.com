Writing as Maintenance, Not Inspiration

I love inspiration. It’s loud and dramatic and shows up with a fresh sack of confidence like, “Yay! We’re geniuses today.”

But… if I waited on inspiration to actually write regularly, I’d maybe have three paragraphs a month and a lot of very tidy notebooks. Most of the work happens in the boring middle place, when I’m not sure what I’m doing, but I show up anyway. Because here’s the truth that took me way too long to internalize:

Your Muse will not make house calls

If you want to find her, you have to put on real pants and go look for her. Like an adult, or something. Lame.

Maintenance is the part of writing that keeps the creative engine from seizing up. It’s not glamorous, and it doesn’t feel like “Art” with a capital A, but it’s the thing that makes art possible. Think less “lightning strike” or "glitter bomb!" and more “oil change,” or "tire rotation." The point isn’t perfection. The point is motion. Keep the lanes moving at all costs.

So here’s a Writing Maintenance Checklist for days when your brain has the creative horsepower of wet cardboard (apologies for the automotive metaphors I'm about to butcher all to Hell):

Mechanical Checks

  • Open the document you’ve been avoiding. (Yes, that counts as progress.)
  • Fix one ugly sentence. Just one. The others get prettier as a result, but you can still fix the next-nasty one later.
  • Read yesterday’s work out loud and circle the spots that make you cringe. There are also tools that read your work aloud; though, don't spend too much time finding the "right" voice (they all kinda suck).
  • Name the problem. If you can diagnose it, you can fix it later.

Fuel System

  • Refill your idea tank.
    • Read a page of something good. Watch a scene you admire.
    • Take exactly one note.
    • Honestly (and yes, I have admitted this to Laura): Period pieces on BritBox are excellent for this (the Keira Knightley Pride and Prejudice or the 2008 Sense & Sensibility) are the perfect way to watch three-act story structure unfold.
  • Write a question about your latest piece. Don’t answer it yet. It will answer itself when you're done and struggle with "what's wrong with my story?!"

Alignment & Tires

  • Move something from Point A to Point B.
    • A character goal.
    • A plot beat.
    • A metaphor.
    • A comma.
    • Let the em dashes live, screw the haters!
  • Re-confirm where the scene is supposed to land. And if it doesn't deserve to live, euthanise it lovingly.

Preventative Care

  • Stop before you hate it.
  • Leave yourself a runway into tomorrow.
  • Celebrate the tune-up.
    • Future-you will notice the smooth ride.
    • Clean writing is something you can continue living within.

Maintenance writing doesn’t feel heroic. It feels like sweeping the floor. But a swept floor is a space where something can happen. Creativity likes clear paths; it can't stand obstacles, and it abhors debt. It loves momentum. And it desperately needs it when we show up, muttering all the while, “Fine, but I’m not happy about this.”

That’s the trick. Do the small things that keep the machine humming. And when inspiration finally sticks her head in the door, she doesn’t have to shout over the sound of a stalled engine. She can just hop in and hit the gas.
