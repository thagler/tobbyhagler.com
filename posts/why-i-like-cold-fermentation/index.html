Why I Like Cold Fermentation

Cold fermentation is basically giving the dough a long nap in the fridge while the microbes quietly work out their chemistry assignments. It slows everything down, evens out the yeast-versus-bacteria chaos, and lets flavor develop on its own timeline. In practical terms: you mix, you fold, you tuck it in, and then (this is clutch!) you stop hovering. The dough doesn’t need your motivational speeches. It just needs time in the dark, where it can work on itself and become something richer, with more depth.

Some Science From Inside the Fridge

  • Cooler temps favor bacteria -- specifically, Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) -- which deepen acidity and flavor.
  • Enzymes break down starch into sugars, giving yeast something to eat over time.
    • Amylase breaks down flour starches into sugars for yeast and bacteria fuel (too much of this and you get hooch, btw).
    • Proteases snip gluten proteins into smaller pieces, making the dough relax.
    • Maltase and Invertase (yeast enzymes) break down sugars for microbial energy. Scientifically speaking: carbs go in, bubbles come out.
    • There are some others that I always forget about.
  • Gluten continues strengthening even without kneading.
  • Overproofing is less likely because yeast slows down. The yeast and LAB balance is easier to maintain.
  • And the best part: the dough becomes easier to shape thanks to that chilled elasticity.

All that complexity is earned by basically doing nothing, which is my favorite thing to do. But it requires patience. Time bends, stretches, and ignores linear causality, especially in the presence of carbs.

Cold Fermentation, Patience, and Resilience

Writing a novel (or any piece of fiction, really) is very similar. I drafted The Grove of Tau Ceti like I was on a timer, convinced speed equaled progress. In fact, I kept a calendar with word counts and deadlines, and I refused to miss those targets. That's the only way I got the first draft written at all.

But after the first big rise came the chill phase: the months of querying, the rejections, the no-responses, the “This isn’t quite right for our list” that all feel like your starter refusing to dome. The book's publication wasn’t broken. It was fermenting. Slowly. Very slowly. Microbial geologic-time slowly.

Cold fermentation reminds me that waiting isn’t the absence of progress. Things develop in the quiet. The kitchen may look still, the bowl may look unchanged, but chemistry is in session. So when I put a dough to rest (or put a draft in a drawer), I try to remember: the rise doesn’t need permission, it just needs time.
