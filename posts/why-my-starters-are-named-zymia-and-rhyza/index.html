Why My Starters Are Named Zymia and Rhyza

Zymia and Rhyza aren’t my first starters. The earlier attempts… didn’t make it. Not because the microbes failed, but because I didn’t yet understand the science of fermentation and commitment. You can’t rush a starter. You can’t ignore it, then hope it forgives you. I had to learn patience and the discipline to make small, incremental changes in order to test how they respond over time.

So when I finally made two starters that wanted to survive, I named them. In The Grove of Tau Ceti, V’s Druidic belief is that naming something means taking responsibility for it. Starters work the same way: the moment you name them, you’re saying, “Okay. You’re mine to feed. You’re mine to learn.” It turns a jar of flour and water into something that depends on you. They are, very literally, alive, and now that I've named them, they are officially in my charge.

Zymia (from “zymology,” the science of fermentation) is the bold one. Hungry, fast, high-energy, always ready to take over a dough like she’s got places to be. Rhyza (from “rhizome,” hidden growth underground) is slower, steadier, and calmer, which is perfect for enriched doughs that require patience and grace. They have different personalities because they are different ecosystems.

People often ask if their starter is “bad.” Too sour? Slow to rise? Weirdly calm? The truth is, they’re all weird in their own ways. They’re alive. They evolve. And when one fizzles, you try again, because skill learns from failure, not luck.

Naming Zymia and Rhyza wasn’t just sentimental. It was a promise:
I name you, and now I'm responsible for you to grow, survive, and live life to the fullest.
